Why I stopped buying new laptops

Older machines offer more quality for less money
December 15, 2023 Technology 4 min
Table of Contents

  • Laptops don’t change
  • How to make an old laptop run like it’s new

As an independent developer - or office worker if you wish - I always reasoned that I needed a decent & fast computer for work and that I needed for quality. Between 2020 and 2024 I consumed two laptops that I bought new (Apple Macbook Pro) which cost me around 5,000 euros in total. Which means it averages out as a yearly cost of 1250 euros. The average lifespan for a machine is only 2 years.

In 2024 I decided not to buy new laptops anymore. Instead I switched to a second-hand machine that I purchased online for 150 euros which had everything I needed. Including a new battery and a simple hardware upgrade (memory & interal SSD) the total cost for this machine came out at 315 euros. If this machine, with its upgrades, lasts as long as my previous machines, the total cost per year is below 160 euros. I’ll explain my motivation and perhaps inspire you to do the same.

Energy and material use of laptops

Not buying new laptops saves a lot of money but also a lot of resources and environment destruction. According to a recent life cycle analysis, it takes between 3,010 to 4,340 megajoules of primary energy to make a laptop - including mining the raw materials, manufacturing the machine and bringing it to market.1

Each year we purchase between 160 and 200 million laptops. Combining these numbers with the data above, this means that the production of laptops requires a yearly energy consumption of 480 to 868 petajoules which correspondents to between one quarter and almost half of all solar PV energy produced worldwide in 2018 (2,023 petajoules)[^2]. The making of a lpaopt also involves a high material consumption, which includes a wide variety of minerals that may be considered scarce due to different types of constraints: economic, social, geochemical and geopolitical.

The production of microchips is a very energy- and material-intensive process. However that is not the real problem. The high resource use of laptops is also because they have a very short lifespan. Most of the 160-200 million laptops sold each year are replacement purchases. The average laptop is replaced every three years in business or five years elsewhere.[^3] So my own ~2 years per laptop experience is not exceptional.

Laptops don’t change

A study shows that the mass and material composition of all key components - battery, motherboard, hard drive, memory - dit not change signifcantly, even though manufacturing processes became more efficient in energy and material use. The reason is simple: improvements in functionality balance the efficiency gains obtained in the manufacturing process. Batter mass, memory and hard disk drive mass decreased per unit of functionality but showed roughly constant totals per year. The same dynamic explains why newer laptops don’t show lower operational electricity consumption compared to older laptops. New laptops may be more energy-efficient per computational power, but these gains are offset by more computational power. Jevon’s paradox is nowhere as evident as it is in computing.

How to make an old laptop run like it’s new

Not buying a new, powerful laptop is not as simple as just buying a used laptop. It’s advisable to:

  1. Upgrade the hardware (RAM, hard drive and CPU, if possible)
  2. Downgrade the software (less system resources in use by the OS for instance)

Use low energy software
